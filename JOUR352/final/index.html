The Mental Health Crisis of America’s Healthcare Workers


Researched/Published By: Adelia McGuire

In most healthcare systems and across all fields of medicine, doctors have been found to have higher rates of depression and anxiety in comparison to the general population and other professional groups.

The work done by physicians, nurses, and other healthcare workers is often glamorized because of the financial stability and rewarding pay which it produces. What many fail to consider is the mental stress which these positions produce and the emotional baggage which healthcare professionals are forced to carry.

Working in the medical field has historically been considered a high-stress profession. For decades, doctors have dealt with higher depression and burnout. This mental health crisis healthcare workers are facing was amplified during the pandemic:

The Burnout of our Nations Healthcare Workers

  • The term burnout describes a state of complete mental, physical, and emotional exhaustion.
  • Burnout is commonly produced when one constantly feels swamped and overwhelmed with stress.
  • Intense, pressure filled environments often create higher rates of burnout.
  • In our nation, healthcare workers are burning out at alarming rates.
  • Healthcare workers who experienced harassment were more likely to report burnout sympotms.
  • A study released by the CDC found that in 2022, 46% of health workers reported feeling burned out.

Comparison

According to, U.S. Health Care Workers Are At Risk For Suicide, published by the Mailman School of Public Health at Columbia University, Health care workers, including registered nurses, health technicians, and health care support workers, are at increased risk of suicide compared with workers in other fields. The article speaks on how up until now very little was known about suicide risks of the approximately 95 percent of health care workers who are not physicians.

To estimate death by suicide rates among U.S. health care workers, researchers studied participants of at least 26 years of age and estimated suicide rates for six health care worker groups—physicians, registered nurses,other health care–diagnosing or treating practitioners, health technicians, health care support workers, and social/behavioral health workers, as well as non–health care workers. Through their analysis researchers found suicide rates per 100,000 person-years of 21 for health care support workers, 16 for registered nurses, 16 for health technicians, 13 for physicians, 10 for social/behavioral health workers, 8 for other health care–diagnosing or treating practitioners, versus 13 for non–health care workers.
